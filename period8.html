Period 8 1945-1980

Period 8 takes on the events that occurred after the World Wars and leads into the Cold War. Other important events such as the Civil Rights Movement and Vietnam War are covered and show the growing power of the United States. (AP: This is 15% of your exam, another big part of the exam so make sure to study!)

Let’s begin with the end of World War II. The conflicts with Germany and Japan dwindled down. Now the war lies between the superpowers of the world: the United States and the USSR.

The United States prospered from the war and it brought the nation out of the Great Depression.

  • GI Bill of Rights 1954 - provided veterans with pensions, government loans, and money to attend college
    • Helped promote economic prosperity
  • National Defense Student Loans 1958 - loans established to encourage the teaching of science and modern foreign languages
    • Passed in response to the launch of Sputnik
  • Sun Belt - region stretching from Florida in an arc across the South and Southwest that saw population growth and industrialization
  • Baby boom - increase in the birth rate that lasted until 1964

After World War II and the invention of nuclear warfare, the USSR decided to develop their own to rival the US. Tension grew as the two superpowers grew in status, thus beginning the Cold War (1945 - 1991).

  • Not an actual war, just a bunch of tension (both political and economic) between the US and Soviet Union
  • Iron Curtain - introduced by Winston Churchill to describe the Soviet domination of Eastern Europe

Foreign policies remained a big part during this era as the US had to deal with the rise in nuclear warfare and the effects of World War II.

  • Truman Doctrine 1947 - President Harry Truman’s policy of providing economic and military aid to any country threatened by communism
  • Marshall Plan 1948 - program designed to promote the economic recovery of Western Europe with massive amounts of US financial aid
  • Berlin airlift 1948 - response to Soviet Union blocking West Berlin
    • Supplies were sent to East Berlin by American and British airplanes
  • North Atlantic Treaty Organization (NATO) 1949 - a mutual defense alliance among the nations of Western Europe, also to contain communism
  • Southeast Asia Treaty Organization (SEATO) 1955 - alliance formed under Eisenhower to prevent Soviet invasion of Southeast Asia and the Pacific
  • SALT I (1972) and II (1979) - treaty between the US and USSR to limit offensive nuclear weapons and defensive antiballistic missile systems
    • However, the US Congress did not approve the second treaty because the USSR invaded Afghanistan
  • Detente - President Nixon’s policy of relaxing tension between the US and Soviet Union; he visited both the Soviet Union and China to smooth the tension
  • Nixon Doctrine - President Nixon’s policy of requiring countries threatened by communism to assume most of their military burden, with the US offering political and economic support
  • Space Race
    • Sputnik I - first artificial satellite launched into space by the Soviets and marked the beginning of the space race
    • Apollo 11 - first spaceflight that landed humans on the moon by the Americans in 1969
  • Nuclear Arms Race
    • Hydrogen bomb 1952 - first hydrogen bomb, which was 1000 times more powerful than the atomic bomb
      • America had their bomb tested in 1952 while the Soviet Union tested theirs in 1953
  • Containment of communism
    • Vietnam War 1954-1975 - after French colonists were forced out of Vietnam, it was divided into 2 nations
      • The North communist regime ultimately attacked the South and tried to take over
      • Increased involvement of American troops
      • The war ended with the victory of the North, so both fell under communism
      • Tet Offensive 1968 - Vietcong (Vietnamese communist rebel in South Vietnam) and North Vietnamese attack throughout South Vietnam that caused public opinion in the US to turn against the war
      • My Lai 1968 - Village in South Vietnam where US troops massacred 350-500 innocent women and children
        • Increased public opposition of the war
      • Vietnamization - President Nixon’s policy of gradually removing US troops in the Vietnam War
        • Finally withdrew in 1973
  • Korean War 1950-1953 - war started by North Korean forces crossing the 8th parallel and invading South Korea
    • United States backed South Korea while China backed North Korea
  • Domino theory - the idea of if a nation falls under communist control, nearby nations will also fall under communism
  • Tonkin Gulf resolution 1964 - After President Johnson claimed North Vietnamese forces attacked US boats in the Gulf of Tonkin, Congress basically gave the president a “blank check” to do whatever necessary to stop communism
  • War Powers Act 1973 - law that prevented the president from involving the US troops into wars without congressional authorization; mandated the president to give 48 hour notice of using a troop and cannot be more than 60 days

The rise in communism led to many Americans becoming afraid of radicalism and communist regimes. The Red Scare began as the United States became increasingly wary.

  • House Un-American Activities Committee (HUAC) - congressional committee created in 1938 that began investigating suspected communists right after WWII
  • Alger Hiss - Former State Department official who was convicted of supplying information to the Soviets
    • Discovered by the House Un-American Activities Committee in Congress
  • Julius and Ethel Rosenberg - husband and wife who were executed in 1953 for passing information about the atomic bomb to the USSR
  • McCarthyism - described the tactic of making accusations of corruption or disloyalty without evidence and no regard for civil liberties
    • Started by Joseph McCarthy, who was a Republican Senator
    • McCarthyism ended with the Army-McCarthy Hearing in 1954, where he accused a soldier of being a communist
  • National Security Act 1947 - reorganized the military under one Department of Defense and established the National Security Council and Central Intelligence Agency (CIA)

The Vietnam War caused quite a stir in America as many were against such violence, especially when there were domestic issues that plagued the nation.

  • Students for a Democratic Society 1960 - left-wing student organization founded to attack Americans materialism and work for social justice and civil rights
    • Associated with the Anti-War Movement in the late 1960s
  • Kent State 1970 - university in Ohio where students were shot and killed by National Guard Troops during a protest against the Vietnam War
  • Pentagon Papers 1971 - classified government documents on the Vietnam War were leaked to the New York Times by Daniel Ellsberg

The Cold War extended to include Latin America, where the US tried to keep communism regimes out (which ended up straining the relationship between the two).

  • Organization of American States 1948 - organization composed of most of the nations of North America, South America and the Caribbean
    • Designed to fight communism in the western hemisphere
  • Bay of Pigs 1961 - invasion of communist-controlled Cuba by Cuban exiles who were supported by the CIA
    • The failure of the invasion led to the embarrassment of President Kennedy and the US
  • Cuban Missile Crisis 1962 - a military crisis over a secret Soviet attempt to deploy missiles in Cuba
    • Closest the Cold War came to ever becoming a real war
    • US threatened the USSR to take the missiles out of Cuba or they would fire
  • Mann Doctrine 1964 - US foreign policy under President Lyndon B. Johnson that called for stability in Latin America rather than political and economic reform
  • Fidel Castro - dictator who took control of Cuba and ruled it as a communist regime

Seems like the United States couldn’t keep itself from getting involved in pretty much every area of the world. Problems also came up in the Middle East as the United States increased its involvement and also had a bad desire for oil.

  • Suez Crisis 1956 - confrontation between Egypt on one side and Britain, France, and Israel on the other side after Egypt tried to nationalize the Suez Canal
    • United Nations played a role in forcing Britain, France, and Israel to withdraw
  • Eisenhower Doctrine - policy passed by President Eisenhower that provided military and economic aid to Arab nations to help defeat communist rebellions
  • Yom Kippur War 1973 - After Egypt and Syria attacked Israel, the Israeli military defeated the Arab armies
    • US support of Israel led to oil shortage for US since Arab nations refused to sell it to US
  • OPEC (Organization of Petroleum Exporting Countries) - economic alliance of oil-producing countries, mostly Arab, that became powerful enough in the 1970s to control oil prices by controlling oil supplies
    • They placed an embargo on all nations allied with Israel
    • US was hard hit as oil prices went up
  • Camp David Accords 1979 - treaty between Israel and Egypt that was negotiated by President Carter
    • Under the treaty, Israel would return occupied Egyptian land and Egypt would recognize Israel as a nation
    • Also known as one of the few things Carter actually did (and it got him a Nobel Peace Prize)
  • Carter Doctrine 1979 - policy that the US would use force to repel any nation that attempted to take control of the Persian Gulf
    • Was passed in response to the USSR invasion of Afghanistan

There’s a hefty load of foreign problems for the United States, but it also has its fair share of domestic issues too. The 1960s became a period for fighting against oppression of minority rights and a rise in liberalism to promote equality. The Civil Rights Movement consisted of civil rights activists who utilized a variety of strategies (legal challenges, direct action, nonviolent protests, etc.) to combat racial discrimination.

  • Rosa Parks - African American who refused to give up her seat for a white man on a bus in Montgomery, Alabama
    • Helped start the boycott of buses
  • Martin Luther King Jr. - Baptist minister and civil rights leader who was committed to nonviolence and established the SCLC
    • Southern Christian Leadership Conference 1957 - became the backbone of the movement to achieve civil rights through acts of nonviolence
  • Student Nonviolent Coordinating Committee 1960 - organization formed to give young blacks a great voice in the Civil Rights Movement
    • Organized black voter registration, sit-ins, and freedom rides
  • Sit-ins - the act of taking a seat in a segregated area to protest racial segregation
    • Especially popular in Greensboro, North Carolina in 1960
  • Freedom rides - effort to achieve integration of bus terminals by riding a bus throughout the South
  • March on Washington 1963 - led by Martin Luther King Jr. to Washington D.C. to pressure Congress to pass civil rights legislation
    • It is where he delivered his famous “I Have A Dream” speech
  • Freedom Summer 1964 - effort by civil rights groups in Mississippi to register black voters
  • Brown v. Board of Education 1954 - pivotal case that overturned the “separate but equal” idea from Plessy v. Ferguson - see cases page
  • Little Rock Nine - 9 black students were not welcomed to go to high school that was to be desegregated
    • President Eisenhower ordered the 101st Airborne Division to escort the students to school safely
  • Executive Order 9981 - order passed by President Truman that desegregated the US Armed Forces
  • Civil Rights Act of 1957 - first civil rights legislation since Reconstruction
    • Created the US Commision on Civil Rights and the Civil Rights Commission of the Justice Department
  • Civil Rights Act of 1964 - law that banned segregation in public facilities and forbade employers to discriminate on the basis of race, religion, sex, or national origin
    • Title VII - provision that guarantees women legal protection against discrimination
  • Voting Rights Act of 1965 - law that banned unjust restriction on voting and authorized federal supervision of elections in areas where black voting had been restricted

The movement for equality wasn’t entirely violence-free. The Black Power Movement rejected nonviolent acts and preferred to use violence to get their point across.

  • Watts 1965 - neighborhood in Los Angeles, California where race riots broke out
    • Resulted in millions of dollars of damage and 28 deaths
  • Malcolm X - member of the Nation of Islam and activist for black separatism
  • Black Panthers - revolutionary organization founded in 1966 that endorsed violence as a means for social change
  • Race riots 1968 - nationwide reaction in more than 100 cities to the assassination of Martin Luther King Jr.
  • George Wallace - Alabama governor who represented “white backslash” to the Civil Rights Movement
    • He opposed racial desegregation and ran for president

The Civil Rights Movement focused mainly on African Americans, but it was also a time that promoted the rights of other minority groups as well.

  • American GI Forum 1948 - organization formed by Mexican American veterans to fight against discrimination and provide support for veterans and Hispanics
    • Helped to end the segregation of Hispanic children in schools through the West and Southwest of America
  • The Feminine Mystique 1963 - novel by Betty Friedan that challenged women to move beyond being a suburban housewife
  • Equal Pay Act 1963 - forbade gender-based discrimination of people performing equal work for the same employer
  • National Organization for Women (NOW) 1966 - organization founded to fight discrimination against women
  • Stonewall Riot 1969 - riot at a gay bar in New York City that was raided by police
    • Helped lead to the gay rights movement
  • Equal Rights Amendment 1972 - amendment that would give women equal rights under the law
    • Failed to be ratified by the required 38 states
  • Cesar Chavez - a leading Mexican American civil rights activists of the 1960s
  • American Indian Movement (AIM) 1968 - militant Indian movement that used confrontation to obtain social justice
  • Wounded Knee 1973 - site of the 1890 massacre of the Sioux by federal troops
    • Was occupied by AIM in 1973 and they insisted that the government honor the treaty from the past

The rise in liberalism didn’t end at the promotion of minority rights. The people urged the government to help out in the times after the war.

  • Great Society 1964 - social program by President Johnson to fight the war on poverty, protect civil rights, and fund education
  • War on Poverty 1964 - program to help Americans leave poverty through education, job training, and community development
  • Medicaid 1965 - health insurance program for the poor and provides states with money to buy health care for people on welfare
  • Medicare 1965 - health insurance program for the elderly and disabled and provides government payment for health care by private doctors and hospitals

Radicalism also played a role during this area as some claimed that there wasn’t enough being done to transform the racial and economic status of the United States.

  • Students for a Democratic Society
  • Berkeley Free Speech Movement 1964-65 - student groups that insisted on their right to political activity on campus

American society was transforming during the 60s as young people began to reject social, political, and economic values of the previous generation and started a sexual revolution and state of informality.

  • Counterculture - rejected the competitiveness and materialism of America and wanted peace, love, and freedom instead
    • Associated with hippies
  • Beat Generation - writers, poets, and artists in the 1950s who rejected traditional values and wanted nonconformity and sexual experimentation
    • Ideas were showcased in their works

Here’s a quick plug about immigration as the laws changed once again after World War II!

  • Immigration and Nationality Act 1965 - abolished the national origins quota system (put in place in 1924) and gave preference to skilled workers

America also focused on the environment! Industrialization created problems and people were becoming increasingly aware of them.

  • Silent Spring 1962 - book by Rachel Carson that warned of the misuse of pesticides and their negative effects on the environment
    • Credited with starting the modern environmental movement
  • Earth Day, April 22, 1970 - international day to celebrate and be aware of the global environmental issues
  • Environmental Protection Agency (EPA) 1970 - Federal agency created to control pollution and protect the environment (as the name would suggest)
    • If this sounds familiar, you’ve probably heard quite a bit about it from the news and how funds may be cut
  • Clean Air Act 1970 - federal law designed to control air pollution
  • Three Mile Island 1970 - site of a nuclear power accident in Pennsylvania
    • Led to radioactive gases and almost created a nuclear meltdown

A period in American history would not be complete without some problems in domestic politics! There were arguments and polarization over social issues that put a divide between conservatives and liberals.

  • New Right - conservative movement that was against liberal political and social reforms in the 1960s
    • Wanted less government intervention and a return to traditional values
  • Watergate Scandal 1972 - scandal in the Nixon administration that began with a break-in at the Democratic National Committee headquarters
    • President Nixon tried to cover up the incident and a whole case expanded on his knowledge of the incident and whether he knew of it or not
    • U.S v. Nixon - see cases page
    • This led to Nixon’s resignation in 1974
  • Abortion became a touchy subject
    • Roe v. Wade 1973 - ruled that women have the right to choose abortion - see cases page
    • Right-to-Life Movement - anti-abortion movement
  • Phyllis Schlafly - New Right activist who protested against the women’s rights movement
    • Opposed the Equal Rights Amendment

Bottom Line: It seems to be one war after another in the 20th century. The United States has successfully come out of World War II as a recognized superpower, but their growth only created conflicts with the rest of the world.

Domestic issues also plagued America as people pushed for equal rights that were not available to them. Some fights were won, but that shouldn’t be the extent to which progress is made.

Liberalism rose in the 1950s and 60s, ushering in social change, but this didn’t last very long.

Conflicts with the world continued and conservatism began to take rise towards the 1980s.
