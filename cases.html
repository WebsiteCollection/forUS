Court Cases

Court cases are a pretty big part of U.S. History and some turning points occur because of them (ex: segregation). It’s good to know the major ones and some are quite interesting. (They can also be used as evidence on essays or debates).

Here we’ll be outlining major court cases that are brought up in history and a couple courts that have stood out, including the basics (what is this even about?) and the context (how am I supposed to know when this was?).

There are also be links for each case to its respective page on Oyez (if available) if you would like more information!

Prominent courts in U.S. History

Marshall Court (1801 - 1835)
A.K.A the first and most major court you’ll hear about

  • Chief Justice John Marshall
  • Preferred federal rights over states’ rights (pretty much Federalists)
  • Expanded the power of the Supreme Court (judicial review!)

Warren Court (1953 - 1969)
A.K.A the really liberal one during the Civil Rights Movement

  • Chief Justice Earl Warren
  • Protected the rights of the accused and minorities

Prominent court cases in U.S. History

Marbury v. Madison - 1803 - Marshall Court

A.K.A. the one that introduced judicial review

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • First instance in which a law passed by Congress was declared unconstitutional
    • Secretary of State James Madison refused to seat four judicial appointees that were approved by the Senate
    • Expanded the power of the Supreme Court and established judicial review
  • Context:
    • AFTER the Election of 1800 (during Jefferson’s presidency)
    • Ties into midnight judges (appointed by John Adams)

McCulloch v. Maryland - 1819 - Marshall Court

A.K.A. the early one about states’ rights

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Upheld right of Congress to establish the Bank of the United States
    • Expanded power of the federal government over that of the states
  • Context:
    • James Monroe is President during this period
    • Era of Good Feelings

Dartmouth College v. Woodward - 1819 - Marshall Court

A.K.A. the one about private vs. public spheres when it comes to colleges

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Dealt with the application of the Contract Clause of the Constitution to private corporations
    • New Hampshire tried to make the college a public institution
    • Supreme Court held the sanctity of the college’s charter that predated the Constitution
  • Context:
    • Era of Good Feelings

Gibbons v. Ogden - 1824 - Marshall Court

A.K.A. the one that dealt with the Commerce Clause

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Dealt with interstate commerce between two states
    • Gave power to the government to regulate navigation
    • Expanded federal power as it was part of the Marshall Court
  • Context:
    • Presidential Election of 1824
    • John Quincy Adams
    • Andrew Jackson
    • Henry Clay
    • Corrupt Bargain

Cherokee Nation v. Georgia - 1831 - Marshall Court

A.K.A the one where the Court couldn’t do much for the natives

  • Basics:
    • Sought a federal injunction against laws passed by the state of Georgia
    • Ruled that the Cherokee Nation was dependent of the United States
    • Case was not heard
  • Context:
    • During Andrew Jackson’s presidency
    • People wanted to expand west and natives prevented this

Worcester v. Georgia - 1832 - Marshall Court

A.K.A. the one that decided only the federal government could deal with the natives, not the states

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Upheld the idea that Cherokee Indians made up its own nation holding distinct sovereign powers
  • Context:
    • During Andrew Jackson’s presidency
    • People wanted to expand west and natives prevented this

Dred Scott v. Sandford - 1857 - Taney Court

A.K.A. the one that solidified the idea that slaves are property (yikes)

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Dred Scott was a slave who was taken across state borders, even to free states
    • Sued his owner for freedom
    • Court claimed that slaves were property and went further to say that it was the job of the government to protect people’s property
    • Denounced the idea of free and slave states (voided Missouri Compromise of 1820)
    • Added tension between the North and South
  • Context:
    • Before the Civil War
    • After the Compromise of 1850

Pollock v. The Farmers’ Loan and Trust Co. - 1895 - Fuller Court

A.K.A. the one that claimed that taxes had to be uniform

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Voided portions of the Wilson-Gorman Tariff Act of 1894
    • Declared federal income tax unconstitutional because it was unapportioned
    • Unsettled by 16th Amendment (give power to “lay and collect taxes on income”)
  • Context:
    • Gilded Age/Progressive Era

Plessy v. Ferguson - 1896 - Fuller Court

A.K.A. one of the worst decisions ever

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Legalized segregation
    • “Separate but equal”
    • Went against the 14th Amendment (citizens deserve equal rights)
  • Context:
    • After Reconstruction (there were still problems)

Insular cases - 1901

A.K.A. the one about what to do when the U.S. gets involved in imperialism

  • Basics:
    • About the status of U.S. territories gained in the Spanish-American War
    • Constitutional rights extend to all places under American control
  • Context:
    • After the Spanish-American War

Northern Securities Co. v. United States - 1904 - Fuller Court

A.K.A. the one against the rich people

  • Basics:
    • Northern Securities Co. established a railroad monopoly
    • Case was to dissolve the company
  • Context:
    • Progressive Era
    • Trust busting

Muller v. Oregon - 1908 - Fuller Court

A.K.A. the one that took a step forward in labor legislation

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Justified sex discrimination and labor laws
    • Allows the passing of laws to protect the health of workers
  • Context:
    • Progressive Era

Schenck v. United States - 1919 - White Court

A.K.A. keep your opinions to yourself

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Freedom of speech
    • Violation of the Espionage Act of 1917
    • Is the U.S. allowed to violate the 1st Amendment during times of war? (spoiler: yes)
  • Context:
    • World War I

Adkins v. Children’s Hospital - 1923 - Taft Court

A.K.A. we took a step backwards in labor legislation

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Minimum wage for women violated the liberty of contract
    • Protected by the due process clause of the Fifth Amendment
  • Context:
    • Progressive Era

Korematsu v. United States - 1941 - Stone Court

A.K.A. the one where the Japanese had it rough

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Fred Toyosaburo Korematsu violated Executive Order 9066
    • Supreme Court believed in protecting the nation against potential security risks rather than protecting individual rights
  • Context:
    • Executive Order 9066
    • After Pearl Harbor
    • World War II

Brown v. Board of Education - 1954 - Warren Court

A.K.A. the one that made up for the mistake that was Plessy

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Overturned Plessy v. Ferguson
    • Declared segregation in public schools as unconstitutional
    • Reinforces Equal Protection Clause in 14th Amendment
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement

Gideon v. Wainwright - 1963 - Warren Court

A.K.A. where felons have rights too

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Require states to provide counsel for criminal defendants who cannot afford it
    • Relates to the 6th Amendment
    • Extends rights to criminals
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement

Escobedo v. Illinois - 1964 - Warren Court

A.K.A. where suspects have rights too

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Criminal suspects have a right to counsel during police interrogations
    • Relates to the 6th Amendment
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement

Griswold v. Connecticut - 1965 - Warren Court

A.K.A. the one about privacy and birth control

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Constitution protects the right to privacy
    • Dealt with contraceptives and marital privacy
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement

Miranda v. Arizona - 1966 - Warren Court

A.K.A. where suspects have rights too part two

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Miranda Rights (what police always say when making an arrest)
    • Protects against self-incrimination
    • Related to 5th Amendment
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement

Roe v. Wade - 1973 - Burger Court

A.K.A. the one about abortion (and the one people want to overturn)

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Legalized abortion
    • Enforced women’s rights
  • Context:
    • Civil Rights Movement and Women’s Rights

U.S. v. Richard Nixon - 1974 - Burger Court

A.K.A. the one where the President messed up

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Nixon was heavily involved in the Watergate Scandal
    • Did executive privilege mean immunity from judicial review? (spoiler: no)
    • Led to Nixon’s resignation and release of tapes and documents
  • Context:
    • Watergate Scandal

Regents of University of California v. Bakke - 1978 - Burger Court

A.K.A. the one that claimed reverse racism is an actual thing when it was just bitter opposition

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Bakke rejected from the medical school in UC Davis
    • Claims of reverse racism (white opposition to equality)
    • Was it a violation of the 14th Amendment? (spoiler: yes)
  • Context:
    • Towards the end of the Civil Rights Movement

Bush v. Gore - 2000 - Rehnquist Court

A.K.A. the one where the Electoral College didn’t match up with popular vote

[More info]

  • Basics:
    • Gore had more popular votes and was predicted to win until Bush gained more electoral votes with Florida (w/ Gov Jeb Bush)
    • Stopped the manual recounting of presidential ballots
    • Bush was declared the winner of the election
  • Context:
    • Election of 2000
